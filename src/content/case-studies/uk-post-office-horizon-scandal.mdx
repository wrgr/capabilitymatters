The Post Office's Horizon system invented shortfalls that weren't there, and rather than doubt the software, the institution prosecuted the people it accused — around 900 sub-postmasters over two decades, some imprisoned, some driven to suicide, in what is now called the widest miscarriage of justice in British history. Engineers knew about the bugs the whole time. The failure ran through the prosecutors and the courts, each treating 'the computer said so' as authoritative because no one had the standing or expertise to challenge it. Horizon is the book's case for what institutional deference to a flawed algorithm actually costs.
