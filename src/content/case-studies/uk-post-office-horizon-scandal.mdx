The Post Office's Horizon system invented shortfalls that weren't there, and rather than doubt the software, the institution and, on its evidence, other prosecutors pursued the people it accused — more than 900 wrongful convictions over two decades (about 700 prosecuted by the Post Office itself), some imprisoned, some driven to suicide, in what is now called the widest miscarriage of justice in British history. Engineers knew about the bugs the whole time. The failure ran through the prosecutors and the courts, each treating 'the computer said so' as authoritative because no one had the standing or expertise to challenge it. Horizon is the book's case for what institutional deference to a flawed algorithm actually costs.
