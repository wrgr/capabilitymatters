Peter Pronovost's central-line checklist has five items, and not one of them was news to the physicians it governed. The question was never what to do — it was whether it got done every time, and whether anyone junior could say so when it didn't. Keystone paired the checklist with something harder to engineer than a list: a nurse's standing authority to halt the procedure when a step was skipped. Across 103 Michigan ICUs, bloodstream infections fell to near zero and stayed there for a decade. The capability that mattered wasn't knowledge — it was permission, built into the system.
